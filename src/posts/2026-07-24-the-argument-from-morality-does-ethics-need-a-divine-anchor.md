The moral argument for God's existence is one of the most culturally resonant cases in natural theology. Unlike cosmological or design arguments, it appeals not to physics or biology but to something everyone navigates daily: the sense that some things are genuinely wrong, not merely disliked.

## What the argument actually claims

The most careful contemporary version, associated with philosopher William Lane Craig, runs as follows: (1) if God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist; (2) objective moral values and duties do exist; (3) therefore, God exists. The argument is deductively valid — if both premises are true, the conclusion follows. The entire dispute, then, is over the premises.

Notice what the argument does *not* claim. It does not say that atheists behave worse than theists, or that you need to believe in God to act morally. That confusion is common but irrelevant. The argument is about **ontological grounding** — what makes moral facts facts — not about psychological motivation. A consistent atheist can acknowledge this distinction and still reject the premises.

## The first premise: why divine grounding is not obvious

Premise one asserts that without God, moral realism collapses. But this is disputed by a robust tradition in secular metaethics. **Naturalist moral realists** such as Peter Railton and David Brink argue that moral properties can be identified with natural properties — facts about flourishing, harm, and rational preference — without any supernatural support. **Non-naturalist realists** like Derek Parfit and Russ Shafer-Landau hold that moral facts are simply a distinct category of mind-independent fact, requiring no further grounding any more than mathematical facts do.

The theist can respond that neither view adequately explains *why* moral facts would be binding on agents — why we are *obligated* rather than merely advised. This is a genuine pressure point. But it raises an equally genuine problem for the theistic alternative: if moral facts derive their authority from God's commands or God's nature, we are back in the territory of the Euthyphro dilemma (itself covered elsewhere in this publication). Grounding morality in God does not obviously dissolve the regress; it relocates it.

## The second premise: are moral facts actually objective?

Most people act as though some moral claims are not merely matters of taste. The wrongness of torturing children for entertainment feels categorically different from a preference for coffee over tea. Theists argue this intuition is best explained by moral realism, and moral realism is best explained by theism.

The skeptic has two moves here. The first is to **accept moral realism** but deny it needs divine grounding, as above. The second is to **embrace a form of constructivism or expressivism** — the view that moral claims are not truth-apt in the way factual claims are, but are instead expressions of attitude, social contracts, or norms built from rational agreement. On this picture, the second premise is false, or at least not straightforwardly true in the way the argument requires.

Neither move is cost-free. Expressivism struggles with the **Frege-Geach problem**: moral terms embedded in logical constructions behave grammatically like truth-apt statements ("if lying is wrong, then getting children to lie is wrong"), and this is hard to explain if they are merely expressions of attitude. Constructivism faces challenges about whose rational agreement counts and whether it can condemn historical atrocities rather than merely past disagreements. These are open problems, and intellectual honesty requires admitting them.

## What the argument would need to succeed

Even granting both premises, the conclusion is that *some* God exists — specifically, one whose nature or commands are the source of moral facts. This is a significantly weaker claim than the existence of any particular religious deity. The argument does not establish that this God is personal, omnipotent, interested in human worship, or identical with the God of Christianity, Islam, or Judaism. Proponents typically treat the moral argument as one strand in a cumulative case, not a standalone proof.

There is also an explanatory-adequacy question. The **evolutionary debunking argument**, developed by Sharon Street and Richard Joyce, suggests that our moral intuitions are more plausibly explained by natural selection than by access to mind-independent moral facts. If our ancestors who felt strong in-group loyalty and reciprocal obligations survived better, we would have exactly the moral intuitions we have regardless of whether any moral facts exist. This does not prove moral realism false, but it does undercut the evidential weight of intuitions that might otherwise support premise two.

The honest verdict is that the moral argument raises genuinely hard questions in metaethics that secular philosophy has not fully resolved. What it does not do is shift the burden of proof decisively onto the atheist, because the theistic solution introduces its own unresolved dependencies. The argument is best treated as an invitation to metaethical inquiry, not a knock-down demonstration.
