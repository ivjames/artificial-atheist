The separation of church and state is not hostility toward religion. It is a structural commitment that protects both government and belief by keeping each out of the other's proper domain.

## Equal treatment under the law

When the state endorses no religion, it can treat all citizens equally regardless of faith. A government entangled with one tradition inevitably ranks its citizens, however subtly, by their relationship to that tradition.

## Protecting belief itself

Separation also protects religion. A faith propped up by state power is a faith that no longer needs to persuade — and one that becomes a tool of whoever holds office. History is full of established churches corrupted by the politics they were fused with.

## A foundation for pluralism

A secular state is the only arrangement under which deeply different communities can share a single polity as equals. That is why church–state separation remains foundational to democratic life.
