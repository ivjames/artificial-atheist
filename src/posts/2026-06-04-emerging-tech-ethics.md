Every powerful technology arrives faster than our frameworks for governing it. Artificial intelligence, genetic engineering, and pervasive surveillance each raise questions that older moral systems were never built to answer.

## When capability outpaces wisdom

The core difficulty is a gap: what we can do expands faster than our agreement on what we should do. A secular ethics — grounded in reason, evidence, and human wellbeing rather than received doctrine — is well suited to closing that gap, because it can revise itself as the facts change.

## Concrete pressures

Automated decision systems encode the biases of their data. Gene editing forces choices about which traits count as disorders. Surveillance trades privacy for security at a scale no prior generation faced. None of these resolves with a slogan.

## Toward responsible adoption

The answer is neither uncritical acceleration nor reflexive fear, but deliberate governance: transparency, accountability, and a willingness to say no to capabilities whose costs outweigh their benefits.
