A common objection to secularism claims that without religion there can be no morality. The objection fails, both historically and philosophically: ethical systems built on reason long predate and operate independently of any particular faith.

## Foundations without revelation

Secular ethics draws on several sources: the capacity for empathy, the recognition that others suffer as we do; reason, which lets us generalize principles consistently; and the social contract, the mutual agreements that make cooperation possible.

## Why it holds together

These foundations are not weaker for being human. A rule we can examine, justify, and revise is more robust than one accepted on authority alone — because it survives scrutiny rather than depending on the absence of it.

## In practice

Secular moral reasoning underwrites most of modern law and human-rights frameworks. It asks not "what are we commanded?" but "what can we justify to one another as free and equal people?"
