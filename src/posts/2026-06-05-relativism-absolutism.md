Are moral truths the same everywhere, or do they depend on culture and circumstance? The debate between ethical absolutism and ethical relativism is one of the oldest in moral philosophy.

## The absolutist case

Absolutism holds that certain moral principles are universally true regardless of who believes them. Cruelty for its own sake, on this view, is wrong in every society — and the fact that some cultures have practised it does not make it right for them.

## The relativist case

Relativism counters that moral judgments are products of culture and history, and that imposing one society's norms on another is itself a kind of arrogance. What looks like universal morality, the relativist argues, is often just one culture universalizing its own habits.

## A middle path

Many philosophers seek ground between the two — defending a small core of universal protections while accepting genuine variation in how the good life is lived. The disagreement persists precisely because both positions capture something real.
